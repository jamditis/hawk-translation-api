`★ Insight ─────────────────────────────────────`
This analysis sits at an interesting intersection: the corpus is effectively a ground-truth dataset of actual translator behavior, which may confirm, contradict, or fill gaps in the prescriptive style guide. The most valuable findings will be where the two diverge — because divergence reveals either a guide rule that isn't being followed (inconsistency) or a real-world practice that hasn't been codified yet (gap).

# Hawk STNS Spanish style guide: corpus analysis report

**Source:** 657 terminology pairs, 38 style observations, 506 professionally translated articles
**Prepared for:** Yuli Delgado, Spanish Translation News Service

---

## 1. Confirmed rules

The following existing style guide rules are well-supported by corpus evidence.

**Attributive verb variety.** The guide's list of attribution verbs — *expresó, mencionó, comentó, afirmó, declaró, manifestó, señaló, explicó, añadió, sostuvo* — is confirmed and actively practiced. The corpus shows translators rotating through these verbs with register awareness rather than defaulting to *dijo* alone. One analysis notes that *afirmó* appears more frequently than *dijo*, the inverse of English journalism's reliance on "said." The variety is not mechanical rotation — translators match verb to context, using *explicó* for elaborations, *señaló* for pointing to data or documents, *declaró* for formal official statements.

**"Mil millones" for billions.** The corpus confirms this rule without exception. One observation explicitly flags that *billón* means 10¹² in Latin American Spanish and should never be used for the English "billion." All large dollar amounts use *mil millones* or spell out *millones*, e.g., *$3.3 mil millones*, *$25.5 mil millones*.

**US-style number punctuation.** Commas as thousands separator and periods as decimals are consistent across all articles — $1,276.50 formatting confirmed. One observation notes this is a deliberate, audience-appropriate choice for NJ readers and should be codified explicitly rather than left implicit.

**Spanish state names in body text.** *Nueva Jersey* appears in every article that names the state. *Nueva York*, *Dakota del Norte*, and other Spanish-form state names follow the same pattern. The corpus confirms that *NJ* as an abbreviation is limited to headlines and official publication names — never in running prose.

**Acronym expansion formula.** The guide's formula — full Spanish name + (por sus siglas en inglés) — is confirmed as the dominant pattern on first use for government and health agency acronyms. Examples from the corpus: *Centros para el Control y la Prevención de Enfermedades (CDC, por sus siglas en inglés)*, *Acción Diferida para los Llegados en la Infancia (DACA, por sus siglas en inglés)*, *Servicio de Inmigración y Control de Aduanas (ICE, por sus siglas en inglés)*.

**Measurements in US units.** No metric conversions appear. US measurements are retained as-is for NJ-audience journalism, confirmed across every article that contains measurement data. One observation notes that time-based regional distances (*a unas 30 millas y 50 minutos en carro al sur de Montclair*) are acceptable alongside mileage for practical context.

**"Humanitarian parole" = *permiso humanitario*.** Confirmed. No alternative translations appear in the corpus for this term.

## 2. Rule gaps

These patterns are consistent throughout the corpus but absent from the existing style guide.

**"Estados Unidos" spelled out in body text.** The corpus overwhelmingly shows *Estados Unidos* written in full in body copy — not *EE. UU.*, not *EEUU*, not *EUA*. This is the actual practice, but the existing guide only specifies the *EE. UU.* abbreviation format without stating the body-text default. The rule needs to be written as: spell out *Estados Unidos* in body text; *EE. UU.* (with spaces after each period, per RAE) is acceptable in headlines when space is tight and on subsequent references when a long article makes full repetition awkward.

**Time and date formatting.** The corpus shows consistent use of *p.m.* and *a.m.* with lowercase letters and periods (*las 11:59 p.m.*). Dates follow the Spanish convention: day + *de* + spelled-out month + *de* + year. Neither rule appears in the existing guide.

**Straight quotation marks.** Every article in the corpus uses standard straight quotation marks, not Spanish angle quotes (« »). This is consistent with AP Spanish style for US-market publications and should be stated explicitly. The guide currently specifies the colon-introduce rule and period-after-question-or-exclamation rule but says nothing about which quote character to use.

**Bill numbers and legislation.** Legislative identifiers (*A1475*, *S-3947*) are kept in their English alphanumeric format throughout. This is not addressed anywhere in the current guide.

**Anglicisms used as common nouns.** When English loanwords enter running Spanish text as nouns, the corpus adds a Spanish accent mark if phonetically needed and an optional parenthetical gloss on first use. The clearest example: *escuela chárter (autónoma)*. "Charter" becomes *chárter* with an accent mark; the parenthetical *(autónoma)* clarifies the concept for readers unfamiliar with the US education system. The guide has no rule for this class of words.

**Proper names never translated.** Publication names, brand names, government programs, and official organization names stay in English regardless of context: *NJ FamilyCare*, *NJ.com*, *STEMCivics*, *The New York Times*. The guide doesn't state this.

**"$X millones" vs. "X millones de dólares" — and the prohibition on mixing $ and *dólares*.** The corpus reveals two distinct acceptable forms for large dollar amounts: *$20 millones* and *20 millones de dólares*. One observation documents the same article using both to describe the same figure, which is clearly an error. Separately, one instance of *$2,000 dólares* redundantly combines the $ sign with the word *dólares* — this is incorrect. The guide mentions US punctuation style for numbers but doesn't specify which form to use for large amounts or ban the $ + *dólares* combination.

**Cents as *centavos*.** Sub-dollar amounts use the word *centavos* rather than the ¢ symbol. Not in the guide.

**"Condado" for county.** The translation is consistent and universal (*condado de Somerset*, *condado de Bergen*) but the guide doesn't include it.

**"Estado Jardín" for Garden State.** The corpus shows this as an established house-style term, used by multiple translators across different articles — not a one-off choice. It should be codified.

**Institutional title capitalization.** The corpus capitalizes *Gobernador*, *Legislatura*, and similar titles when used as proper institutional references, but uses lowercase when they appear descriptively before a name. This reflects standard Spanish usage but the guide doesn't address it.

## 3. Inconsistencies

These are places where translators diverged from the guide, from each other, or within single articles.

**"EE. UU." vs. "Estados Unidos."** This is the most significant divergence between the guide and corpus practice. The guide specifies *EE. UU.* (with spaces) as the standard, but the corpus shows body text almost uniformly using *Estados Unidos* in full. One analysis notes: "No abbreviated forms appear anywhere in the excerpts." A separate analysis shows *EE. UU.* appearing in headlines and formal title contexts (*Candidatos del Senado de EE. UU.*, *Representante de EE. UU. Andy Kim*). The guide and actual practice describe different defaults. The guide should be updated to match what translators are actually doing: *Estados Unidos* in full in body text, *EE. UU.* in headlines.

**ICE Spanish expansion.** Two different Spanish translations of ICE appear across articles: *Servicio de Control de Inmigración y Aduanas* and *Servicio de Inmigración y Control de Aduanas*. The second form is the widely recognized official Spanish name and should be locked as the canonical translation.

**Acronym formula consistency.** While the guide's formula (*Spanish expansion + (ACRONYM, por sus siglas en inglés)*) is the dominant pattern, the corpus shows three different approaches in use:
- Full formula with *por sus siglas en inglés*: the standard
- Spanish translation given in parentheses after the English name (reversing the order): observed for NOAA
- Spanish translation only, no acronym retained: observed for several organizational names

The position of the acronym also varies — sometimes before *por sus siglas en inglés* (*ICE, por sus siglas en inglés*), sometimes after a conjunction (*o COAH por sus siglas en inglés*). The rule needs tighter wording: Spanish expansion first, then *(ACRONYM, por sus siglas en inglés)* — always in that order.

**ACA without the *siglas* formula.** The Affordable Care Act is translated as *Ley de Atención Médica Asequible* in the corpus but without the *por sus siglas en inglés* treatment. Given the rule exists, ACA should get it.

**Dollar amounts: "$X millones" vs. "X millones de dólares."** As noted above, one article uses both forms to describe the same figure. Since both forms appear consistently elsewhere — just not in the same sentence — this is an inconsistency that needs a tie-breaking rule.

**"Estado Jardín" capitalization.** Two capitalizations appear: *el estado del jardín* (lowercase, treated as a descriptive phrase) and *el Estado Jardín* (title case, treated as a proper nickname). Standardize on the latter when used as a proper epithet for New Jersey.

**SNAP expansion.** One article introduces SNAP by spelling out the full Spanish name first, then using the acronym, but omits *por sus siglas en inglés*: *formalmente llamado el Programa de Asistencia de Nutrición Suplementaria. El programa, ampliamente conocido como SNAP*. This diverges from the guide's formula. Well-known acronyms (SNAP, FBI) probably don't need the full *por sus siglas en inglés* treatment on every use, but the guide should say so rather than leaving it to translator judgment.

## 5. Recommended guide additions

The following are concrete rule statements ready to add to the style guide.

---

**"Estados Unidos" in body text**
Spell out *Estados Unidos* in all body text. Use *EE. UU.* (with a space after each period) only in headlines where space is tight or on subsequent reference in long articles where repeated full use is awkward. Never use *EEUU*, *EUA*, or *EE.UU.* (without spaces).

**Time and dates**
Times use a 24-hour or 12-hour format with *a.m.* and *p.m.*, lowercase, with periods: *las 3:00 p.m.*, *las 11:59 p.m.* Dates follow the Spanish convention: *el 15 de marzo de 2025*.

**Quotation marks**
Use straight quotation marks (" "), not Spanish angle quotes (« »). This follows AP Spanish style for US-market publications.

**Bill numbers**
Keep legislative identifiers in their English alphanumeric format: *A1475*, *S-3947*. Do not translate or reformat.

**Anglicisms used as common nouns**
When an English word has entered common use as a noun in US-context Spanish journalism, spell it with any required Spanish accent mark and provide a parenthetical Spanish gloss on first use. Example: *escuela chárter (autónoma)*. Do not italicize anglicisms. Do not use this treatment for proper names (see below).

**Proper names: never translate**
Official names of publications, government programs, organizations, and brands stay in English regardless of context: *NJ FamilyCare*, *NJ.com*, *Medicaid*, *Real ID*, *The New York Times*. If clarification is needed, add a brief parenthetical: *el programa NJ FamilyCare (seguro médico estatal)*.

**Large dollar amounts: one form only**
Write large dollar amounts as *$X millones* or *$X mil millones* — dollar sign, numeral, then the spelled-out unit. Do not use *X millones de dólares*; do not add the word *dólares* after the $ sign. Incorrect: *$2,000 dólares*. Correct: *$2,000*.

**Cents**
Use the word *centavos* for sub-dollar amounts. Do not use the ¢ symbol.

**"County" → "condado"**
Always translate *county* as *condado*: *condado de Somerset*, *condado de Bergen*.

**"Garden State" → "Estado Jardín"**
When used as a proper epithet for New Jersey, write *el Estado Jardín* — capitalized, as a proper nickname. Lowercase (*el estado jardín*) is incorrect.

**ICE: canonical translation**
The canonical Spanish expansion for ICE is *Servicio de Inmigración y Control de Aduanas*. On first use: *Servicio de Inmigración y Control de Aduanas (ICE, por sus siglas en inglés)*. On subsequent reference: *ICE* alone.

**Acronym formula: order and exceptions**
On first use, the Spanish expansion always comes first, followed by the acronym and the *siglas* note in parentheses: *[Spanish name] ([ACRONYM], por sus siglas en inglés)*. The acronym appears inside the parenthetical — never reversed. Only retain the acronym if it will be reused in the article; if not, use only the Spanish translation.

Exception: widely recognized acronyms (SNAP, FBI, AIDS, COVID-19, LGBTQ+) do not need the *por sus siglas en inglés* treatment. Use judgment — if a general NJ Spanish-reading audience would not recognize the acronym without explanation, use the full formula.

**Attribution verbs by register**
Use attribution verbs to match the register of the quote, not to avoid repetition mechanically:
- *dijo*: neutral baseline for direct quotes
- *afirmó*: emphatic personal statements
- *declaró*: formal or official statements (press releases, legal proceedings)
- *señaló* / *señala*: when pointing to data, a document, or a written source
- *explicó* / *explicaron*: elaborations or context-giving remarks
- *comentó*: informal or conversational quotes
- *informó*: officials relaying factual information
- *sostuvo* / *argumentaron*: formal positions, legal arguments — not casual statements
- *advirtió*: warnings

Do not repeat the same verb in adjacent paragraphs. Do not rotate mechanically regardless of context.

**Institutional title capitalization**
Capitalize *Gobernador*, *Legislatura*, *Corte Suprema*, *Departamento*, and similar titles when used as the proper name of a specific institution or role. Use lowercase when the term is used descriptively before a name: *el gobernador Phil Murphy* (descriptive) vs. *el Gobernador anunció* (standing in as a proper institutional reference).
